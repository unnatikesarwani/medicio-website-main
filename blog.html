Introduction

Neuroplasticity is the brain’s ability to reorganize itself—crucial in childhood when the brain is most adaptable.

Key Points

  • Forms new neural pathways through learning, experience, or injury.
  • Helps children with Cerebral Palsy, Autism, and Developmental Delays.
  • Sensory integration, motor retraining, language enrichment rely on neuroplasticity.
  • Repetition + meaningful engagement strengthen connections.

At Raahat Therapy

  • Task-specific movement for motor delays.
  • Play-based cognitive tasks for memory/attention.
  • Intensive therapy blocks for faster rewiring.

Final Thought

The brain is flexible and resilient—change is expected with the right therapy.

Introduction

Speech therapy is not limited to sound correction—it supports feeding, communication, cognition, and social skills.

Key Points

  • Feeding & Swallowing: Oral-motor exercises, sensory feeding.
  • Language Development: Play-based vocabulary, comprehension, storytelling.
  • Social Communication: Eye contact, tone, turn-taking, social stories.
  • Alternative Communication: AAC, PECS, sign language for non-verbal children.

Empowering Families

Home programs, caregiver coaching, and school collaboration.

Conclusion

Speech therapy helps children express, connect, and participate fully.

Introduction

Play is the brain’s most natural way to learn—forming the root of all early childhood development.

Key Points

  • Activates prefrontal cortex for emotional regulation & decision-making.
  • Builds problem-solving and executive function.
  • Reduces anxiety and builds trust.

Examples in Therapy

  • Obstacle courses for balance and planning.
  • Pretend play for narrative and speech skills.
  • Sensory bins for tactile exploration.
  • Board games for impulse control and turn-taking.

Final Thought

Play makes therapy effective, enjoyable, and deeply engaging.

Introduction

Modern therapy blends hands-on care with smart tech for better engagement and tracking.

Introduction

ASD affects communication, behavior, and sensory processing—each child needs individualized support.

Key Points

  • Challenges: Social skills, language delays, repetitive behavior, sensory issues, emotional regulation.
  • OT: Fine motor, daily living, sensory integration.
  • Speech Therapy: Verbal + non-verbal skills, AAC, PECS.
  • ABA: Evidence-based behavior development.
  • Parent Training: Routines, life skills, IEP/IFSP support.

Conclusion

Autism is not a limitation—right support leads to growth and independence.

Introduction

Food influences behavior, learning, mood, and movement through the gut-brain connection.

Key Points

  • 70% of immunity + neurotransmitters in the gut.
  • Issues: Sensory food aversions, constipation, nutrient deficiencies, sugar crashes.
  • Our Approach:
    • Food desensitization
    • Nutrition coaching
    • Parent workshops
    • Coordination with OT, speech, and behavior therapy

Conclusion

Good nutrition helps children learn better, move better, and feel balanced.

Introduction

Executive function helps children plan, focus, remember, and self-regulate—like the brain’s control tower.

Key Points

  • Core skills: Working memory, cognitive flexibility, inhibitory control.
  • Common signs: Forgetfulness, incomplete tasks, emotional outbursts, difficulty following directions.
  • Success Example:
    • OT for planning/visual schedules
    • CBT-based coaching for impulse control
    • Parent guidance for routines
    • School collaboration for support

Conclusion

With structured support, executive function can dramatically improve.

Introduction

Gait retraining restores walking ability and confidence in neuro rehab.

Key Points

  • Challenges: Foot drop, weak trunk control, fear of falling, muscle stiffness.
  • Therapies:
    • Parallel bars
    • Body-weight–supported treadmill training
    • Mirror therapy
    • Resistance-based step training
  • Success Example: A stroke patient walked again after 6 weeks.

Conclusion

Every step matters—gait training builds independence and hope.
